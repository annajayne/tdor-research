> So far, only one person has been arrested in connection with these incidents, but authorities assure that the investigation remains active and more arrests could be made in the coming days.
> 
> The first person arrested in this case was identified as Juan Camilo Muñoz Gaviria, alias Teta, who was detained on April 30 during a joint operation between the Prosecutor's Office and the National Police. Read: One of the suspects in the brutal murder of Sara Millerey arrested.

> Happy, joyful, kind-hearted—those are just a few of the adjectives that best described Sara Millerey González Borja, the trans woman brutally murdered in a river in the municipality of Bello, Antioquia. 
> 
> Sandra Milena Borja, Sara's mother, is navigating these days in a kind of "sea" of memories. Surrounded by picture frames containing her daughter's best postcards, Sandra delves into memory to bring her beloved daughter's life back to the present.
> 
> “This is her graduation photo. It was in 2017. She was a girl who loved to write. Very devoted to God. These are the little photos of her I remember,” she says, with that sadness that only mothers feel when they lose a child. It's that absolute desolation and sadness that parents feel when the premise that says they, the children, are responsible for burying their parents, and not the other way around, is shattered.
> 
>  “This was on a birthday,” she adds, holding back tears. “She was very cheerful, very happy. She liked to write about God and the Virgin Mary. I have some writings here, if you want, I can show them to you,”  she tells RED+ NOTICIAS journalist Maicol Buitrago, who in his interview delved into the notebooks containing Sara's unpublished letters and writings, a previously unknown side of the trans woman the entire country is talking about today.
> 
> The texts reveal the other Sara. The Sara who dedicated a few hours of her time to writing letters addressed to God, to the Virgin Mary, and to life, using a black pen, the kind that leaves your fingers stained with ink.

> On a day marked by emotion, diversity and the firm defense of human rights , the public hearing of Bill 122 of 2024, known as the "Comprehensive Gender Identity Law" or "Sara Millerey Law" , was held on April 24, 2025, in the House of Representatives.
> 
> This legislative initiative aims to effectively guarantee the fundamental rights of people with diverse gender identities in Colombia , such as health, education, justice, identity, among others.
> 
> The event, hailed as a success by various sectors, opened with a deeply meaningful symbolic act: a minute of silence in memory of Sara Millerey , a trans woman murdered on April 7 and whose name gives the bill its title.

Under the bridge on the Medellín-Bogotá highway , near Solla, they painted a mural in honor of Sara Millerey , the trans woman who died in La María Hospital after being brutally beaten and thrown into the La García ravine in Bello .

Her story has sparked a stir and a wave of backlash on social media . International media outlets have taken an interest and have come to record the location from which the young woman was rescued, in the Paya Rica neighborhood of this municipality in the northern Aburrá Valley, and have interviewed her family.

The final act of solidarity, recognition, and rejection of her death was captured on this mural, passed by thousands of vehicles every day. The message is clear and direct: "Hate is killing us ." This is the phrase that accompanies the image of Sara, wearing a blue dress, and the years 1993, when she was born, and 2025, when she died. All of this is set against the colors of the LGBTIQ+ community flag.
